Nine raw tables → a MySQL star schema → SQL views → an interactive Tableau story a sales manager can explore, surfacing that the top 9.4% of customers drive 38.3% of revenue.

Problem
A retailer's sales data sat spread across many raw operational tables, unusable for anyone trying to answer a business question. The goal: turn that raw data into a governed warehouse and a self-serve dashboard so a sales manager could explore revenue by product, region, customer, and promotion without writing a single query.
Data
Microsoft's AdventureWorksDW2017 sample. Nine tables: one Internet Sales fact of 60,398 order lines, plus eight dimensions (date, product, subcategory, category, customer, geography, territory, promotion). Covers online sales from 2010 through 2013.
Approach
Loaded the nine tables into a MySQL database and modeled them into a star schema (a central sales fact surrounded by dimension tables). Wrote four SQL views to pre-shape the analytics layer (sales by category & month, sales by territory, customer segments, and promotion effectiveness) so the reporting logic lives in the database, not the dashboard. Connected MySQL live to Tableau and built the views into an interactive Tableau story.
Result
Two findings the dashboard surfaces. Revenue is highly concentrated: the top 9.4% of customers (those spending $5K+) generate 38.3% of total revenue, a clear long-tail pattern with direct implications for where retention effort should go. And on promotions: the Volume Discount drove a genuine average-order-value lift (~$947 vs. ~$469 for no discount, across ~2,000 real orders), while several promotions that looked stronger on paper turned out to be fixed-price product bundles rather than a true promotional effect, a distinction worth catching before anyone acts on it.
Next steps
The warehouse is read-only and batch-loaded. Next: automate the load as a scheduled pipeline, add incremental refresh so new sales flow in without a full reload, and layer row-level security so different regional managers see only their territory.

Six forecasting methods, scored on data they never saw. Auto-ARIMA projected Walmart's revenue within ~4%, half the error of the best regression.

Problem
Quarterly revenue forecasts drive inventory, staffing, and budget decisions, so the method behind them matters. How accurately can we project a large retailer's revenue 1–2 years out, and which forecasting approach should a planning team actually trust?
Data
Walmart's public quarterly revenue, 2006 Q1 – 2025 Q3 (~79 quarters). A strong upward trend plus a hard Q4 holiday spike every year: the two patterns any model has to handle.
Approach
Built a ladder of six methods: regression with seasonality, linear, and quadratic trend; fixed and auto-selected ARIMA; and a two-level hybrid I engineered (a quadratic regression with an AR(1) model layered on its residuals, motivated by leftover autocorrelation in the residual ACF). Each was scored on a held-out 19-quarter window no model saw in training, so the comparison reflects real forecasting skill, not memorized history.
Result
Auto-ARIMA was the most accurate at ~4% MAPE, roughly half the error of the best regression (~8%). The detail I'd flag in an interview: my two-level hybrid had the tightest in-sample fit of any model but landed mid-pack out-of-sample, a clean illustration that fit and forecasting accuracy are different things, and that residual diagnostics reveal what a tidy-looking regression hides.
Next steps
The model is univariate by design: it learns only from revenue's own history. Next: fold in macro covariates (consumer spending, CPI), test prediction-interval coverage rather than point error alone, and extend the benchmark to other retailers.

A product can run 93% negative this month while its lifetime average moves less than 3 points. This finds those products from review text alone.

Problem
The rating on a product page is a lifetime average, which makes it structurally blind to recent decline. By the time it moves, the damage is months old and already sitting in returns and refunds. And nobody can read 74,258 products' reviews to compensate.
Data
Amazon Fine Food Reviews: 568,454 reviews across 74,258 products, 1999–2012.
Approach
For each product with enough history (≥40 reviews over ≥12 months, 1,150 of them), Fisher's exact test compares the negative rate in its last 15 reviews against its own baseline. Benjamini–Hochberg correction across all 1,150 tests controls the false discovery rate. Survivors are passed to an LLM API (Gemini) that returns a fixed schema (what changed, since when, what to check, category) so the output is a structured action, not a paragraph. Sentiment labels come from a companion TF-IDF classifier, which is what lets the same logic run on support tickets and survey text where no star ratings exist.
Result
107 products flag at raw p < 0.05, but with 1,150 tests, ~58 of those are expected from chance alone. After FDR control at q = 0.05, five survive, and each is externally verifiable: a pet-food safety episode (0% → 67% negative, q = 0.0018), three recipe or supplier changes, and one fulfillment failure where a coffee sampler shipped as loose pods in paper bags. Every row carries its q-value and window length, so the list can be audited rather than just trusted.
Next steps
The window is a fixed 15 reviews, so across the five alerts "recent" spans anywhere from 13 days to 312. Holding review count constant holds statistical power constant, but changepoint detection would replace the fixed window. The since field is model-inferred and can differ from the computed window start; both ship as separate columns. The corpus ends in 2012, so this is retrospective, though the detection logic is date-agnostic and runs unchanged on a live feed.
